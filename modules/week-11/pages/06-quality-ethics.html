Quality + ethics

Treat geocoding as a data quality problem. Your map is only as good as your inputs and your reference data.

Common problems: address table

  • Street name variations (Fifth Avenue vs 5th Ave)
  • Misspellings
  • Wrong address numbers
  • Missing or incorrect direction (N/S/E/W)
  • Wrong street type (Rd vs St)
  • PO Boxes (not a street location)
  • Incorrect ZIP codes
Fix Clean and standardize before geocoding. Otherwise you’ll spend time rematching and guessing later.

Common problems: reference database

  • New streets not included yet
  • Street name changes or closures
  • Missing address ranges or gaps
  • Out-of-date ZIP/place attributes

Reference data can be “right” for one year and wrong for the next.

How to assess quality

Match rate

Match rate (hit rate) is the percentage of input records that were matched.

Rule of thumb: 85–87% can be “good” for large datasets — but always inspect what didn’t match.

Positional accuracy

Positional accuracy measures how far the geocoded point is from the true location.

Interpolation-based addresses can be off by tens of meters (or more), especially on long segments.

Check yourself: what’s “good enough” depends on…?

Answer: your use case. A citywide hotspot map might tolerate block-level error; a parcel-specific enforcement action might not.

Online geocoding services (pros + cons)

Pros

  • Fast and easy to access
  • Often free or inexpensive for small jobs
  • Useful for quick exploration

Cons

  • Privacy/confidentiality (you’re sending addresses to a third party)
  • Accuracy varies; error handling may be opaque
  • Terms of service and rate limits
