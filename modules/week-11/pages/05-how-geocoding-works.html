How geocoding works

Geocoding is a three-stage process: preprocessing → matching → interpolating.

Keep in mind Many results are approximations along a street segment—not rooftop or parcel locations.

1) Preprocessing (parsing + standardizing)

Parsing breaks an address into components. Standardizing makes those components consistent (ordering, abbreviations, missing values).

  • Parsing example: is St. “Street” or “Saint”?
  • Standardizing example: convert 5th Ave, Fifth Avenue, 5TH AV into a consistent form.
Check yourself: parse this address

Address: 125 E Oak St, Apt 2, Pittsburgh, PA 15213

  • Number: 125
  • Prefix: E
  • Street name: Oak
  • Type: St
  • Unit: Apt 2
  • City / state / ZIP: Pittsburgh, PA 15213

2) Matching (candidate streets + match scores)

The geocoder compares your parsed address to the reference database and proposes one or more candidates.

  • A match score reflects how well the text matches (street name, type, ZIP, etc.).
  • You can set a threshold to accept or reject low-confidence matches.
  • If multiple candidates tie, you may need manual review or better input data.

A stricter threshold can increase confidence but reduce match rate.

3) Interpolating (estimating the point location)

Linear interpolation estimates where the address falls within an address range on a street segment.

  • If the reference segment has a range (e.g., 1001–1299), the address number is placed proportionally along the segment.
  • Often, odd numbers are on one side and even numbers on the other (but this is not universal).

Offsetting (making points easier to see)

When multiple points fall on the same street segment, a geocoder can offset them slightly.

  • End offset: moves points away from intersections/endpoints
  • Side offset: nudges points off the centerline to the left/right
