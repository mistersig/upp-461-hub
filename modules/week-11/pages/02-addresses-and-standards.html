Addresses + standards

Before we geocode, we need to ask: what is an address, and who gets counted as “addressable”?

“An address, today, is an identity… In the modern world, in short, you are your address.”

— Deirdre Mask, The Address Book

What’s in an address?

An address is a structured description of location that people and institutions can agree on.

  • Human readable: designed for mail delivery, navigation, and service access.
  • Machine usable: can be parsed into components and matched to reference data.
  • Contextual: what “counts” as an address varies across places and systems.

Why addresses matter (and who gets left out)

Addresses as access

  • School enrollment, voting, utilities, banking
  • Eligibility for services and benefits
  • Being “findable” by institutions

Addresses as power

  • Many people live without formal addresses
  • Invisible to datasets and decision systems
  • Emergency response and recovery can fail
Check yourself: what’s the planning implication?

Prompt: If a community has informal addressing, what might happen when the city tries to allocate resources using address-based data (permits, inspections, service requests)?

Takeaway: Address systems shape who is legible in administrative data — and that affects equity, enforcement, and service delivery.

Addressing “models” (very simplified)

Common in many “Eastern” systems

  • Location described by areas (districts/blocks/buildings)
  • Numbers may not reflect distance along a street
  • Navigation often relies on landmarks + local knowledge

Common in many “Western” systems

  • Street name + address number
  • Numbers often follow a predictable pattern (odds/evens)
  • Supports interpolation-based geocoding on street segments

These are broad generalizations. Real addressing systems are diverse and often hybrid.

Addresses in emergencies

  • Dispatch and routing require fast, unambiguous locations.
  • After disasters, informal or changing addresses can complicate aid delivery.
  • Good addressing + good reference data can save time and lives.

Standards (why they matter)

Standards help different systems interpret the same address consistently.

  • U.S.: FGDC + the National Address Database (NAD)
  • Global: Universal Postal Union (UPU)
  • Geospatial: Open Geospatial Consortium (OGC) (addressing + geocoding service patterns)
Practical point The more your input data matches standard formats, the higher your match rate (and the less time you spend cleaning).
