Week 11: Turn addresses into mappable coordinates — and learn what can go wrong (and how to check your results).

You’ll use these concepts in the in-class geocoding exercise and in your final project workflows when you need to map address-based data.

Learning goals

  • Explain what an address is (and why it matters for institutions and services).
  • Describe the three phases of geocoding: preprocessing → matching → interpolating.
  • Identify the two key data inputs: an address table and a reference database.
  • Assess geocoding quality using match rate and positional accuracy.
  • Recognize privacy and equity considerations when using online geocoding services.

Key idea Geocoding is an estimate. Always validate results before you treat them as truth.
Check yourself: what kind of “address” is geocodable?

Prompt: Which of these can usually be geocoded to a map point?

  • 125 Oak St E, Apt 2, Pittsburgh PA 15213 ✅ (street address with locality)
  • P.O. Box 123, Pittsburgh PA 15213 ❌ (mail drop, not a street location)

Some systems can geocode to a ZIP centroid or a post office, but that’s different from placing a point at a building or parcel.
