Topology

Topology describes spatial relationships that don’t change when shapes are stretched: adjacency, connectivity, and containment.

Planar topology (classic example)

When you model polygons as a planar partition, polygons should not overlap. Shared boundaries matter for area calculations.

Common topology problems

Why you should care: overlaps can double-count area; gaps can “lose” land; misalignment can produce incorrect joins and overlays.

Real errors vs “faux” errors

Real errors break your data model (e.g., polygons overlap when they shouldn’t).

Faux errors often come from scale/resolution differences (e.g., one dataset was digitized at a different scale or generalized differently).

Your job is to decide what matters for the question you’re asking—and what tolerance is acceptable.
