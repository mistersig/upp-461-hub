What is GIS?

GIS is a way to store, visualize, and analyze data that are tied to locations on Earth.

The big idea for Week 2: representation choices (vector vs raster, topology rules, measurement scales) shape what questions you can answer—and how wrong you can be.

Recap from Week 1

  • Coordinate systems matter: you can’t measure distance/area correctly without the right projection.
  • Data models matter: the same phenomenon can be represented multiple ways (and not all are appropriate).

Why this week matters

  • Topology errors can create bogus results (e.g., double-counted areas).
  • Wrong measurement scales lead to misleading summaries (e.g., averaging category codes).
  • File formats + metadata affect whether data are usable at all.

Try it: “What is my phenomenon?”

Before you map or analyze, answer these three questions:

  1. What exists in the real world? (objects, boundaries, surfaces, flows…)
  2. How am I representing it? (points/lines/polygons vs grid cells)
  3. What am I assuming? (crisp boundaries? perfect alignment? stable categories?)

We’ll use these repeatedly in planning applications (zoning, land use, transportation, hazards).
