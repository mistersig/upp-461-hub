1) What is a CRS?

A Coordinate Reference System (CRS) is a “rulebook” that tells your GIS software how to interpret coordinate values and where those coordinates belong on Earth.

A CRS usually includes:

  • An Earth model (via a datum)
  • A coordinate system (either Geographic Coordinate System (GCS) or Projected Coordinate System (PCS))
  • Units (degrees vs meters/feet) and axes definitions
Quick check If a layer “draws in the wrong place” but the attribute table looks right, you might have a CRS mismatch (wrong CRS assigned or data needs projection).

Define vs Project (common confusion)

  • Define: “This dataset’s coordinates are in CRS X.” (no math; just labeling)
  • Project: actually converts coordinates into a new CRS (math happens; geometry can shift)

Mini activity: spot the mismatch

Pick the most likely cause:

  • A web basemap draws fine, but your points show up off the coast of Africa.
  • Your layer says it is WGS 84 but the coordinates look like large numbers such as 1,080,000 and 245,000.
Show suggested answer

Likely mismatch: the layer is labeled with the wrong CRS (it may be a projected system in meters/feet, not degrees). Defining the correct CRS first is often the fix.
