Policy + natural experiments

Why it matters: planning and policy decisions happen somewhere. Spatial analysis links data to where decisions land.

Spatial thinking = policy insight

  • Policies have geographic impacts (who benefits, who is burdened).
  • Access and exposure are spatial (distance, travel time, adjacency).
  • Equity questions often become spatial questions.

Example goal (site selection): Identify priority neighborhoods for a city-owned grocery store under a food equity initiative using spatial data and stakeholder input.

Turning spatial data into policy action

Check yourself: what would you need for the grocery-store question?
  • Demand: population, income, household structure.
  • Access: existing grocery locations, transit, walking distance.
  • Constraints: zoning, parcels, ownership, infrastructure.
  • Stakeholders: community input to validate criteria.

Natural experiments (policy evaluation)

Natural experiments occur when policy variation happens without researcher control (variation across places or time).

  • Examples: changes in benefits, legalization, minimum wage, healthcare expansion.
  • Key idea: you compare outcomes across places/groups exposed vs not exposed (with careful validation).

Policy example: Paycheck Protection Program (PPP)

PPP changed over time (phases) and across lender types—creating potential comparison points.

  • Early phases prioritized speed and larger banks.
  • Later rules aimed to target smaller businesses and underserved borrowers.
Check yourself: what would you validate before calling it a “natural experiment”?
  • Are comparison groups similar enough (or can you adjust)?
  • Is the timing and geography of the policy variation clear?
  • Could other events explain the difference?
