Week 7: Data Preparation & Interoperability — Spreadsheets & data cleaning, Shapefiles, U.S. Census data

Learning goals

  • Explain Extract, Transform, Load (ETL) in a GIS workflow.
  • Clean a spreadsheet so it’s machine-readable and join-ready.
  • Choose the right Census product (Decennial Census vs American Community Survey (ACS)) and interpret Margin of Error (MOE).
  • Join Census tables to boundaries using a stable key (typically Geographic Identifier (GEOID)).
  • Use a normalization checklist (Codd-style rules) to spot table problems before GIS breaks.

Required reading / materials

  • Bolsta dChapter 8 Introduction to GIS

What matters most this week

Data interoperability + interacting with the Census website (access issues noted). Focus: complete the workflow and explain your choices (variables, geography level, join key).

Common pain points we’ll tackle

  • Following the full workflow end-to-end (download → clean → join → map → interpret).
  • Working with U.S. Census data (geographies, GEOID, ACS vs Decennial, margins of error).
  • Codd’s rules / normalization concepts.

Key vocabulary (first-use expansions)

Extract, Transform, Load (ETL)
A workflow for bringing data from a source into a usable analysis-ready format.
Open Geospatial Consortium (OGC)
An international standards organization for geospatial data/services interoperability.
American Community Survey (ACS)
A sample survey that produces estimates (with MOE), not full-count totals.
Topologically Integrated Geographic Encoding and Referencing (TIGER)
Boundary + feature datasets from the U.S. Census Bureau (roads, boundaries, etc.).
Geographic Identifier (GEOID)
A stable code used to join Census tables to boundaries (e.g., tract GEOID).
Margin of Error (MOE)
Uncertainty range around an estimate (ACS uses MOE at a stated confidence level).
Check yourself: What’s the main difference between ACS and Decennial Census?

Answer: Decennial Census is intended to count the whole population once every 10 years. ACS is a sample survey producing estimates over 1-year or 5-year periods, and those estimates have MOE.
